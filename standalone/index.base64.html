They can read your webmail. We can help.

Web-based mail, with all of its advantages, comes with a very serious privacy flaw - all messages are stored in a single central location on the webmail operator's data center. This fact makes these data centers a natural interception point for various undesirable parties and severely risks your privacy as a webmail user.

You can solve this problem and protect your privacy by using secret sharing: Split each message into two shares and the shares through two different webmail providers.

On this site you can encode your own private mail, decode a private mail somebody else sent you, or have a fun time exploring and playing with secret sharing.
This is an open-source project - we'd love you to help us out!

Helping out

The code is designed to be extended and maintained in the form of an open-source project, and as such was designed to accomodate contributors with different levels of technical expertise and commitment. We have identified several areas in which outside help is very welcome:
  • First and foremost, contributors can provide support for additional output languages by collecting reference corpora in these languages and using them to construct Huffman-Markov DictionaryTree data structures. An online tool provided for this purpose can be found at the project page at Google Code.
  • Contributors can also provide integration with additional webmail systems by writing additional implementations of the ClientIntegration class, based on modified versions of the included Gmail support class.
  • Contributors can also provide additional codec algorithms by writing additional implementations of the Codec class. The reasoning that led us to create the reverse Huffman codec might be applicable to other codebook-based compression methods as well. It also seems natural to use standard steganographic methods to embed the secret share in a human-generated "carrier" message.
